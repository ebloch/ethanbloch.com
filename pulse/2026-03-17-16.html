Lead Story

Jensen Huang on GTC, Groq, and Why Agents Need Fast CPUs

Stratechery

Ben Thompson sat down with Jensen right after his GTC keynote. The whole thing is worth reading, but the meat is around agents and inference economics:

Agents need fast single-threaded CPUs. When agents use tools, those tools were designed for humans and run on CPUs. If the CPU is slow, millions of dollars of GPUs sit idle. That's why Nvidia built Vera — not to compete with x86 on cores, but to never throttle the GPU. Three times the memory bandwidth per CPU core of anything else.

Groq makes sense now. Nvidia's acquiring Groq to handle the extreme-latency end of inference (think coding agents where you'd pay 10x for 10x speed). They'll co-process — even disaggregating at the decode level — shipping later this year.

"Our software engineers 100% use coding agents now. Many of them haven't generated a line of code in a while, but they're super productive and super busy."

For Hiro: The whole interview reinforces your "agents need integration" thesis. Also notable: Jensen on why reasoning models changed everything — "Without reasoning, you couldn't ground the generative AI... that allowed us to build tool use."

AI Strategy

The Three-Horse IPO Race

Om Malik

Om cuts through the OpenAI "focus" news. The real story: three AI giants (OpenAI, Anthropic, SpaceX/xAI) are racing to IPO while the window exists. If all three offer 15% of shares, it would equal every dollar raised in American IPOs over the past decade.

Why it matters: Gulf sovereign wealth money is distracted by geopolitics. Public markets in NY/London have to carry the load. The window is short.

The stark contrast: Anthropic's revenue run rate hit $19B (up from $9B at end of 2025). $6B added in February alone — almost entirely Claude Code. OpenAI is doing $25B but was "all over the map" with Sora, Atlas browser, hardware, TikTok-for-AI.

"Anthropic has no friends in Washington. What it has, in the immortal words of Steve Ballmer, is 'Developers, Developers, Developers.'"

Claude Cowork Was an Accident

Latent.Space

Felix Rieseberg (formerly Slack desktop, Electron core maintainer) on how Cowork happened: They noticed Claude Code users doing messy knowledge work, not coding. Built in 10 days with multiple Claude Code instances orchestrating each other.

Key insight on cheap execution: "We used to write memos, specs, debate. Now we just build all the candidates very quickly... whatever is better is what you go with."

Why local matters: "Silicon Valley is undervaluing the local computer." The VM is both a safety boundary and capability unlock — Claude can install tools and run scripts without constant approval. That's the middle ground between "uselessly safe" and "dangerously autonomous."

On skills vs MCPs: Felix is increasingly interested in file-based, text-native interfaces over rigid tool schemas. Skills are portable; MCPs lock you in.

Engineering Practice

Simon Willison on Trusting Agent Output

Simon Willison's Newsletter

Highlights from Simon's Pragmatic Summit fireside chat on working with coding agents:

On trust: "When I worked at a big company, other teams would build services for us and we wouldn't go look at their code... Opus 4.5 was the first one that earned my trust — I'm very confident now that for classes of problems I've seen it tackle before, it's not going to do anything stupid."

TDD for agents: "I've hated test-first TDD throughout my career... Getting agents to do it is fine. I don't care if the agent spins around for a few minutes wasting its time on a test that doesn't work." Tests are no longer optional — they're free now.

Conformance-driven development: Want to implement file uploads? Tell Claude to build a test suite that passes against Go, Node, Django, and Starlette. Now you've reverse-engineered a standard from six implementations.

"Poor quality code from an agent is a choice you make. If I'm a little lazy and there's a refactoring that would take an hour, I'm not gonna do it. If an agent takes an hour but I prompt it and walk the dog? Sure."

Fintech & Markets

Tax Alpha > Market Alpha

Matt Levine / Money Stuff

Levine on a simple investing heuristic: spend 5% more time thinking about taxes and 5% less time trying to beat the market. Why?

Beating the market means competing against the world's best hedge funds and their computers. Reducing taxes means competing against... the IRS. Smart people, but they don't get a 30% bonus when they extract $100M from you.

The strategy: Long-short stock portfolios designed not just to make money, but to generate losses that offset capital gains. If you figure all stocks are roughly the same and have random returns, it's easy to construct a portfolio that earns market returns AND generates tax losses.

"Tax alpha is the most consistent source of outperformance that you can deliver."

Hot Takes

Will AI Kill Spreadsheets?

a16z speedrun

Andrew Chen kicked the hornet's nest: "Anything currently modeled as a spreadsheet is better modeled in code." Spreadsheets are business logic trapped in a grid — no version control, no testing, no modularity.

The counterargument (from a finance guy): "If an MD draws on their iPad 'make a box here, change this EBITDA calc, break out product lines'... they can get exactly what they want in an hour. They don't need to wait 12 months for a SaaS platform to add the feature."

The nuance: There are two kinds of spreadsheet use. "Mini software" (dashboards, trackers) — almost certainly getting replaced. But models you build to develop conviction about inputs? Stickier. The act of building is how you understand the business.

SFUSD's Algebra Plan Misses the Point

GrowSF

Remember Prop G passing with 82% support to bring back 8th grade algebra? SFUSD's proposed plan would require kids to take both regular 8th grade math AND Algebra as an elective — meaning they'd have to give up art, music, or language.
