Apple at 50: Why Integration Still Wins (And What AI Could Change)

Stratechery

Ben Thompson's retrospective on Apple's 50th anniversary makes a compelling argument: Apple has survived five decades by being the only company truly integrating hardware and software. From IBM to Microsoft to Android, no competitor has matched this approach. The company's position looks stronger than ever—the MacBook Neo is winning the low-end, market share keeps growing, and Apple is cleverly aggregating AI providers rather than building its own models.

But here's the real insight: the long-term threat isn't that Apple missed AI—it's that AI could shift where integration matters. If the point of integration moves from device + OS to AI + device, and OpenAI (with Jony Ive's help) can build hardware that makes the traditional smartphone form factor less relevant, Apple's 50-year moat could erode. Apple is already paying retention bonuses to stop OpenAI from poaching iPhone designers.

"Apple, by making everything from operating system to device to chip, is selling a computer that is both higher quality and has higher performance with lower component costs than the alternatives in its class."

For Hiro: Critical for thinking about where value accrues in AI. If Apple can aggregate AI providers and take 30% of subscriptions, the "AI-first fintech" play needs to think hard about device distribution.

The Coding Agent Harness Race: Claude Code Gets Computer Use, Codex Gets Plugins

AINews / ben's bites

This week marked a significant escalation in the coding agent wars. Anthropic added computer use to Claude Code—meaning the agent can now open apps, click through UIs, and test what it built. It's closed-loop verification: code → run → inspect UI → fix → re-test. Available in research preview for Pro/Max users.

Meanwhile, OpenAI shipped a Codex plugin for Claude Code, letting you trigger Codex reviews from inside Anthropic's toolchain. The interoperability is notable: coding stacks are becoming composable harnesses rather than monolithic products. And the open-source Hermes Agent had a breakout week, with users migrating from other setups citing better compaction and faster shipping.

"Cross-agent composition is becoming standard: coding stacks are becoming composable harnesses rather than monolithic products."

For Hiro: The harness/orchestration layer may matter more than picking the "best" model—and the tools are becoming interoperable enough to mix and match.

Tech Stocks Hit Buy Signal as Iran War Crushes Valuations

Bloomberg

The Nasdaq 100 is down 12% from October highs and trading at its narrowest premium to the S&P 500 in eight years—a valuation level that has historically preceded tech outperformance. WTI crude is up 50% for March alone (best month since May 2020) as the Iran war threatens Hormuz shipping. Americans are paying $4/gallon for gas for the first time since 2022.

The market is caught in a tug-of-war: war-driven inflation argues for higher rates, but the growth hit from $100+ oil argues for cuts. European stocks lost €420 billion in March—and just three stocks (LVMH, SAP, Novo Nordisk) account for more than half of that. Asian stocks had their worst month since October 2008.

"We are buyers of Big Tech. All the more so since there are a number of tech stocks whose valuation is below the pandemic trough."

For Hiro: The war is creating both risk and opportunity—and the macro backdrop affects fundraising/growth environment.
