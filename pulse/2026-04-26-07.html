The $100B+ Missing Connectivity Layer (And Why Agents Make It Urgent)

Fintech Business Weekly

Rory O'Reilly, CEO of Knot, makes the case that payments infrastructure has had two great eras — the network era (Visa, Mastercard) and the bank connectivity era (Plaid, Yodlee) — but the third layer is missing: the ability to act inside a user's actual merchant relationships. Not just observe that money moved, but change a delivery address, cancel a subscription, redeem loyalty points, or update payment credentials at Amazon, DoorDash, or Delta.

The essay is sharp on why agents expose this gap. Most agentic commerce products today either do browser automation (brittle, fights CAPTCHAs) or create their own merchant account — but that strips the user of rewards, purchase protections, order history, and their actual relationship with the merchant. O'Reilly calls this the distinction between delegation and substitution. The concept he introduces is "delegated authentication": a trusted system acts on behalf of a user with permission, without pretending to be the user.

The essay argues whoever owns this layer will look, eventually, like the other payment rails — obvious in hindsight, indispensable once built.

"A useful commerce agent has to act through the user's real merchant account, using the user's real payment method, inside the user's real relationship with the merchant. Anything else is not delegation. It is substitution, and that distinction matters."

For Hiro: This maps almost perfectly onto what Hiro will need to build. An AI financial advisor that actually executes — moves money to the right card, cancels forgotten subscriptions, optimizes rewards at checkout — requires exactly this delegated authentication layer. The framing of "passive insight → active execution" is the same arc Hiro is riding. Worth reading in full and thinking about whether this is a partner, a competitor, or infrastructure Hiro should be building on top of.
