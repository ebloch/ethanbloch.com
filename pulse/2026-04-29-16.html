Stablecoins Are Replaying 150 Years of Banking History

Fintech Takes

Alex Johnson's conversation with former Acting Comptroller Mike Hsu frames stablecoins less as a new crypto invention and more as the latest version of old private-money problems: wildcat bank notes, offshore Eurodollars, Libra's failed regulator surprise, and the recurring question of who gets to issue trusted dollar instruments.

The useful lens for Ethan is regulatory path dependence. Supervisors are not reasoning from scratch every time a new product appears; they carry institutional memory from prior failures. That makes stablecoins a product-design and go-to-market problem, not just a reserve-quality problem.

My take: the Circle/Tether split is a nice map of the market. One route optimizes for legitimacy, the other for scale as a fact on the ground. For financial agents, the same tradeoff will show up around custody, identity, and movement of money.

"What examiners worry about today was transmitted to them by someone who learned it from someone who lived through a crisis."

Why it matters: Directly relevant to Hiro/OpenAI personal finance: money movement products inherit regulatory memory, and agentic finance will run into the same trust rails.

Avis Turned a Short Squeeze Into a Weird Non-Dilutive Capital Raise

Bloomberg Money Stuff

Matt Levine walks through the Avis/Pentwater short squeeze: Avis stock ran from about $100 to more than $700, but the company and major holders were constrained by blackout periods and Section 16 short-swing profit rules. Pentwater still sold 4.3 million shares for $1.75 billion, and at least some of the profits may have to be disgorged back to Avis.

The delightful mechanism: Pentwater may keep profits from accounts not matched under Section 16, while Avis gets cash from the matchable trades. That makes the whole episode look like someone else sold into the squeeze and gave the issuer part of the proceeds.

My take: this is a reminder that market structure and legal plumbing often matter more than the headline price move. The trade is not just 'short squeeze'; it is who is allowed to monetize the squeeze, when, and through which account.

"Short squeeze as a service"

Why it matters: Good investing/process story for Ethan: incentives, legal constraints, and account-level structure can dominate the economic outcome.
