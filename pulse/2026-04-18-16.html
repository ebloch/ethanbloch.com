OpenAI Acquires Hiro Finance in Likely Acquihire

TechCrunch / TLDR Fintech

OpenAI has acquired Hiro Finance, the consumer AI financial planning startup you founded after Digit. The team is joining OpenAI as the product shuts down. TLDR's framing: "focused on talent and domain expertise rather than maintaining a standalone consumer app" — classic acquihire language.

Hiro was built around accuracy in financial math and AI-powered personal financial scenario modeling, which maps neatly onto what OpenAI is clearly positioning for in fintech. They're not buying a product — they're buying the belief that AI + personal finance is a real problem worth solving, plus the people who've already tried it once.

This is a fascinating data point for you specifically: your prior company just validated that the space is real enough for OpenAI to want the team. You're now competing (sort of) with OpenAI in the AI financial advisor category — except you're building Hiro, which shares a name. Worth watching how OpenAI deploys this talent.

"Hiro built tools to model personal financial scenarios using AI, emphasizing accuracy in financial math. The deal signals OpenAI's continued expansion into financial use cases, likely focused on talent and domain expertise rather than maintaining a standalone consumer app."

For Hiro: This is your former company. OpenAI's acquihire confirms the AI financial advisor thesis has legs — and that your team's domain expertise is valuable enough to pull into the frontier lab building the models you're building on. Time to move fast.

Derek Thompson: The AI Vibe Shift Has Officially Arrived

Derek Thompson

Thompson identifies a 'double vibe shift' in AI. The first: the frontier labs have gone from racing to release to paranoid caution. Anthropic withheld Claud Mythos — reportedly so good at cyberhacking that existing safety measures aren't sufficient for public release. OpenAI is also restricting access to its most advanced models. The race-to-ship era may be over.

The second shift is more important for investors and builders: the AI bubble narrative has inverted. For a year, skeptics pointed to the massive capex buildout — the largest private-sector infrastructure project in history — as classic bubble behavior. But the data now shows the constraint isn't demand, it's supply. Hyperscalers cannot build compute fast enough to meet white-hot customer demand.

Thompson's new historical analogy: not the 19th-century railroads (which crashed repeatedly), but the dawn of electricity in the early 20th century — builders struggling to keep up with demand while creating enormous financial and political headaches.

"the biggest problem facing AI is not a shortage of demand—that is, a shortage of customers—but rather a shortage of supply—consumer demand is so white-hot that the hyperscalers cannot provide sufficient compute to keep up with customer needs."

For Hiro: The supply-constrained framing matters for Hiro's timing. If AI capabilities are gated on compute, not adoption, the window to build AI financial advisory before the models commoditize the core functionality is shorter than it looks. Build faster.

AI Models & Tools

  • Claude Opus 4.7: Strictly Better at Every Tier, Plus Claude Design Launches

    AINews / Latent Space

    Anthropic dropped Claude Opus 4.7 and it's a clean across-the-board upgrade: every effort level of 4.7 beats the equivalent level of 4.6. The AINews key chart shows 4.7-low is strictly better than 4.6-medium, 4.7-medium beats 4.6-high, and 4.7-high beats 4.6-max. There's also a new 'xhigh' effort level that Claude Code now defaults to. Despite a new tokenizer that can add up to 35% more token usage, overall efficiency gains mean actual token consumption is down up to 50% vs equivalent prior-generation tasks. The vision improvement is notable for agent builders: Opus 4.7 accepts images up to 2,576 pixels on the long edge (~3.75 megapixels), more than 3x prior Claude models. Simultaneously, Anthropic launched Claude Design — a research-preview tool for generating prototypes, slides, and one-pagers from natural language. Multiple observers called it a direct shot at Figma, Lovable, Bolt, and v0.

  • RIP Pull Requests (2005–2026)

    AINews / Latent Space

    GitHub just allowed users to disable pull requests on open source repos for the first time ever. Pete Steinberger's case for 'Prompt Requests' over Pull Requests is gaining ground: no merge conflicts, easier for maintainers to fix the prompt than review the code, less risk of malicious slippage. Aaron Levie framed it: 'the path forward is to make software that agents want.' If Code Reviews are dead and Pull Requests are dying, Git-based workflows may not survive the agent era.

  • Notion's Token Town: 5 Rebuilds to Get Agent-Native Right

    Latent Space

    Notion rebuilt its Custom Agents feature four or five times before shipping — early failures came from no tool-calling standard, short context windows, unreliable models, and too much complexity exposed to the model. The 'Agent Lab' thesis: not just wrapping a model, but understanding how people collaborate and building the right product system around it. Their long-term vision is 'software factories' — agents that spec, code, test, debug, review, and maintain codebases together.

  • Agents Are Doing More Work, Yet Everyone Is Working Harder

    Om Malik

    After a Molotov cocktail hit Sam Altman's gate and bullets were fired at his home, OpenAI is asking people to 'de-escalate' AI rhetoric — while the New Yorker's Kyle Chayka points out Altman himself spent years warning the product could 'most likely lead to the end of the world.'   • Goldman Asks: Is Tech Actually Cheap Now?

    a16z

    After the selloff, tech now trades at roughly a 25% P/E premium over the broader market — down from a much larger historical premium and back to ~2018 levels. Globally, tech P/E is now below consumer staples and industrials. Yet tech EPS growth expectations have been rising through the selloff, from 31% to 43.4% for US IT as of April 9. That's 40% composite EPS growth vs. the broader market's 18.7%. The valuation-vs-fundamentals divergence is striking.
