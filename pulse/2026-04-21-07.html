Tim Cook's Legacy: From 0→1 to $4 Trillion — and Now an AI Question Mark

Stratechery

Ben Thompson delivers his Cook eulogy and it's a masterpiece of nuance. The headline — Cook grew Apple from $297B to $4 trillion — obscures the real story. Cook was the rarest thing in business: a non-founder CEO who inherited a category-defining product at exactly the right moment, and had the operational genius to scale it to an almost incomprehensible level. Revenue up 303%, profit up 354%, an iPhone line scaled to hundreds of millions of units, and a Services business ($436B revenue, $118B profit last year) that Cook essentially invented from the App Store and Google search deal seeds Jobs planted.

But the Cook Doctrine — "own and control the primary technologies behind the products we make" — is now being violated in two directions simultaneously. China owns Apple's manufacturing; Google will power its AI. Thompson's most cutting observation: Apple white-labeling Gemini for Siri wasn't a short-term fix, it was a long-term strategic surrender. Once Gemini works, Apple will never have the stomach to rip it out and rebuild from scratch. Cook steps down at exactly the right moment for his legacy: after Apple's best-ever quarter, before the AI reckoning hits. John Ternus takes the reins with a very clean slate and a very hard question: what does Apple do when the device layer commoditizes and the intelligence layer is owned by someone else?

"I think Apple has made a good decision here for short term reasons, but I don't think it's a short-term decision: I strongly suspect that Apple, whether it has admitted it to itself or not, has just committed itself to depending on 3rd-parties for AI for the long run."

For Hiro: The Cook-to-Ternus transition is a live case study in the "build vs. buy AI" question. If Apple — with $4T market cap and decades of silicon expertise — couldn't get there, what does that say about the moat of companies that own their AI stack? Hiro's thesis has to include an answer to this: what's your defensible AI layer?

Claude Hits $30B Run Rate — Then Starts Locking Out China

The Information / Bloomberg

Two stories colliding today around Anthropic, both remarkable. First, the revenue: Claude's annualized gross sales hit $30 billion — tripling in under four months. Amazon just added another $5B to its investment (on top of $8B previously), with commitments of up to $20B more tied to milestones, plus Anthropic pledging $100B in AWS spend over 10 years. The Amazon deal also unlocks 5 gigawatts of compute capacity, making Anthropic's infrastructure picture look serious.

Second, the crackdown: Anthropic quietly started requiring government-issued photo ID from some customers, effectively shutting out Chinese developers who had been accessing Claude through VPNs, rented US phone numbers, and Singapore bank cards. One Beijing startup co-founder estimated his company was spending $5,000/month across 12 Claude Code accounts. The shutdown is already pushing Chinese developers toward DeepSeek V4 (rumored soon) and a Chinese competitor (Zhipu) building one-click Claude→Zhipu migration tooling. The implication: Anthropic's servers are "melting" from demand. Revenue tripled. They literally can't serve everyone right now, so cutting off policy-violating users is also just capacity management.

"Having Claude Code is like having 50 engineers working for you. And it's much cheaper than hiring 50 engineers."

For Hiro: The $30B run rate number is the most important data point about the state of AI today. Claude Code is driving almost all of it — a coding agent. For Hiro: the question is what the financial advisor equivalent of Claude Code looks like. The Chinese crackdown is also creating a real market opportunity for local model alternatives.
