Oil Surges Past $115 as Iran Conflict Escalates — Fed Rate Cuts Now a Coin Flip

Bloomberg Markets Daily

The Iran conflict entered a new phase with strikes on the South Pars gas field and retaliatory attacks on Qatar and Saudi Arabia. Brent crude touched $119 before settling around $115, while WTI lags at $96 — creating a massive spread as Middle Eastern grades top $150.

The macro fallout is cascading: stocks are down 3.7% since the conflict began, traders are now pricing two ECB hikes this year, and the Bank of England is skipping what was once a near-certain rate cut. The Fed's Powell made clear no cuts are coming until inflation resumes cooling — and some officials are now talking about the next move being a hike.

One bright spot in the chaos: China's surge in renewable capacity is paying off, potentially emerging as a winner from the energy disruption.

"China is light years ahead of the US in terms of energy, and energy is probably the key sector globally."

For Hiro: This is the macro backdrop for everything else. Rising energy costs hit consumer spending, corporate margins, and startup funding environments. For Hiro, it means wealth management conversations shift to inflation hedging and energy exposure.

MiniMax 2.7 Matches SOTA Open Model at One-Third the Cost

AINews

Not two months after their IPO, MiniMax dropped M2.7 — matching Z.ai's GLM-5 on Artificial Analysis benchmarks while costing just $176 to run the full intelligence index vs GLM-5's ~$530. They're hitting 56.22% on SWE-Pro, 57% on Terminal Bench 2, and parity with Sonnet 4.6 in OpenClaw evals.

The interesting angle: they're calling it 'our first model deeply participating in its own evolution,' handling 30-50% of its own development workflow. Early autoresearch vibes.

Also worth noting: Xiaomi's MiMo-V2-Pro scored 49 on the Intelligence Index with 1M context at $1/$3 per million tokens. Chinese open models are getting very competitive very fast.

"M2.7 is our first model deeply participating in its own evolution... capable of handling 30%-50% of the workflow."

For Hiro: Cost efficiency at this level changes the economics of agent deployment. For Hiro, cheaper inference means more agentic features become viable at consumer price points. My take: the 'agents building agents' thread is accelerating faster than expected.
