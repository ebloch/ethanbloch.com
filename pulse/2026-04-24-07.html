Meta Installs Surveillance Software on Employee Computers to Train AI Agents

Platformer

Casey Newton reports that Meta is rolling out a program called the Model Capability Initiative (MCI), which captures mouse movements, clicks, keystrokes, and screen snapshots from U.S.-based employees to train AI agents that can perform computer tasks like humans. Meta CTO Andrew Bosworth described the goal as a future where agents "primarily do the work" while employees "direct, review and help them improve." When one employee asked if they could opt out, Bosworth's reply was blunt: "No there is no opt out on your work provided laptop." (Europeans can relocate — GDPR protects them.)

This is happening alongside a 10% layoff of ~8,000 people — no coincidence. Newton frames it with a sharp historical lens: this is Taylorism applied to knowledge work. A century ago, factory managers broke skilled labor into measurable motions so they could be standardized and automated. Now Meta is doing the same to engineers, designers, and product managers. The connection: Meta paid $14.3B for a 49% stake in Scale AI last year — a company whose entire business model was extracting workflow data from contractors. They just moved that playbook inside.

"The result is that the people who were once entrusted with building the machine have now become raw materials for it. At Meta, that used to be what the users were for. Now it's what the employees are for, too."

For Hiro: This is the sharpest real-world preview of what AI disruption looks like inside a major tech company. The Taylorism frame is the right one to sit with: AI doesn't replace a job all at once — it first makes the job legible to machines, then automatable. The question for any fintech building on AI: are you on the surveillance side or the disrupted side?

OpenAI Launches GPT-5.5 and Turns Codex Into Its AI Superapp

AINews

OpenAI launched GPT-5.5, positioned as "a new class of intelligence for real work," with rollout inside ChatGPT and Codex. API access is delayed pending safety review. The model hits strong agentic benchmarks: 82.7% Terminal-Bench 2.0, 58.6% SWE-Bench Pro, 78.7% OSWorld-Verified. Pricing will be $5/$30 per 1M input/output tokens (standard) and $30/$180 for Pro — double GPT-5.4's rates. The efficiency story is the real hook: GPT-5.5 uses fewer tokens per task than its predecessor, and Sam Altman framed OpenAI as increasingly an "AI inference company."

But the bigger move isn't the model — it's what OpenAI shipped alongside it for Codex. Browser control, Sheets/Slides, Docs/PDFs, OS-wide dictation, and auto-review mode (a secondary "guardian" agent that reduces approval interruptions on longer runs). OpenAI is turning Codex from a coding assistant into a full computer-work agent that handles QA, spreadsheets, presentations, research loops, and overnight experimental runs. The framing is now explicit: Codex is OpenAI's superapp.

"GPT-5.5 (medium) scores the same as Claude Opus 4.7 (max) on our Intelligence Index at one quarter of the cost (~$1,200 vs $4,800) — although Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview scores the same at a cost of ~$900."

For Hiro: The Codex-as-superapp move is the most important product strategy play of this cycle. OpenAI is bundling frontier model capability with an agentic workspace that can operate computers autonomously. This is the platform that will define enterprise AI adoption. The infrastructure you're building on is getting dramatically more capable, but platform lock-in risks are intensifying too.

  • DeepSeek V4: MIT-Licensed 1.6T-Param Model, Aggressive Pricing

    AINews

    DeepSeek answered GPT-5.5 within hours: V4-Pro (1.6T params, 49B active) and V4-Flash (284B/13B active) are both MIT-licensed with 1M token context. The pricing is the real weapon — V4-Flash at $0.14/$0.28 per 1M tokens and V4-Pro at $1.74/$3.48, compared to GPT-5.5's $5/$30. The catch: V4-Pro throughput is currently limited, with DeepSeek explicitly waiting on Huawei Ascend 950 clusters to drop prices further. The open weights plus 1M context plus MIT license makes this the new open-model frontier.

  • Anthropic's Claude Code Regression: What Broke and Why

    TLDR

    Anthropic's postmortem on the Claude Code quality regression (fixed April 20) traced the issue to three separate changes that affected Claude Code, the Claude Agent SDK, and Claude Cowork — the API was unaffected. This is the clearest public window yet into how fragile compound AI systems can be: a regression in one layer silently degraded downstream products. The fact that users noticed before Anthropic did is worth sitting with.

  • Qwen3.6-27B Beats Models 14x Its Size on Coding

    AINews

    Qwen3.6-27B (dense, Apache 2.0) outperforms Qwen3.5-397B-A17B on major coding benchmarks including SWE-bench Verified (77.2 vs 76.2) and Terminal-Bench 2.0 (59.3 vs 52.5) — a 27B dense model beating a 397B MoE. Local community benchmarks put it at $4 in electricity vs $142 in API costs for equivalent workloads. Algorithm improvements are compressing faster than compute is expanding.
