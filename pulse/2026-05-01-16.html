DeepSeek V4 Pushes Near-Frontier Models Toward Commodity Pricing

Simon Willison's Newsletter

DeepSeek released V4-Pro and V4-Flash preview models: 1M-token context Mixture-of-Experts systems under the MIT license, with Pro at 1.6T total parameters and 49B active, and Flash at 284B total with 13B active. Simon's read is that V4-Pro is now the largest open-weights model, while Flash may become interesting for high-end local machines once quantized.

The bigger point is pricing. Flash is listed at $0.14 per million input tokens and $0.28 per million output tokens; Pro at $1.74 input and $3.48 output. If the benchmark claims hold up even directionally, the pressure on model margins and routing strategies gets sharper.

My take: this is another reminder that durable AI product advantage will come less from access to one model and more from orchestration, data context, trust, UX, and domain-specific loops. For personal finance, cheap long-context reasoning is powerful, but integration and correctness are still the scarce pieces.

"DeepSeek-V4-Pro is the cheapest of the larger frontier models."

Why it matters: Relevant to Ethan's OpenAI personal-finance work because model cost curves shape what can be embedded into everyday financial workflows. If near-frontier long-context reasoning gets cheap, the bottleneck moves toward product judgment, permissions, and reliable financial actions.

The OpenAI-Microsoft AGI Clause Appears to Be Dead

Simon Willison's Newsletter

Simon traced the history of the unusual OpenAI-Microsoft clause that would have ended Microsoft's commercial IP rights after AGI. The original concept moved from a mission-language definition, to investor documents reportedly tying AGI to $100B of potential profit, to an independent expert panel structure.

The latest partnership language says Microsoft will have a non-exclusive license to OpenAI IP for models and products through 2032, while revenue-share payments continue through 2030 independent of technology progress. Simon reads that as effectively killing the AGI-triggered commercial cutoff.

My take: the story is less about philosophy and more about institutional maturity. Ambiguous mission clauses are hard to run at trillion-dollar infrastructure scale; predictable commercial rights make the partnership financeable, even if the rhetoric around AGI stays weird.

"As far as I can tell independent of OpenAI's technology progress is a declaration that the AGI clause is now dead."

Why it matters: Directly relevant to Ethan's current OpenAI context: it frames how governance, capital, and product distribution are being normalized as frontier AI becomes more like core infrastructure.
