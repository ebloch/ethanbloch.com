Memory Is the Machine

On my Om

Om Malik has written the definitive explainer for why the Mac mini is sold out until August — and it has nothing to do with Apple Intelligence. The real answer: memory bandwidth is the bottleneck for local AI inference, and Apple is the only consumer hardware company shipping above the ~400 GB/s threshold where local LLMs actually feel real. The math is concrete: a 70B-param model compressed to 4-bit is 35GB. At 100 GB/s, that's 2 tokens/second — you're watching a cursor drool. At M5 Max's 614 GB/s, you get 17 tokens/second — a real conversation. The semiconductor industry has quietly set 300–500 GB/s as the floor for usable edge AI. The M5 Pro hits 307. AMD, Intel, and Qualcomm are 4-5 years behind.

This was a 2020 accident that became a 2026 advantage. When Apple shipped the M1 with unified memory, everyone wrote about Rosetta and CPU performance. The memory architecture was a footnote. Five years later, it turns out that unified memory — shared across CPU, GPU, and Neural Engine, with bandwidth that no PC laptop could touch — is exactly what inference needs. Two other threads: OpenClaw gets a direct name-drop as a driver of Mac mini demand, and an outsized share of the best open-weight models running on these Macs come from China — DeepSeek, Qwen, Kimi — because chip export controls forced Chinese labs to optimize the model side.

"Apple did not win an AI race. Apple made a memory-architecture decision in 2020 that turned out to be the AI race, five years before there was an AI race."

For Hiro: This is the hardware foundation under the personal AI thesis. Local inference at the edge — on your device, with your data — requires this bandwidth threshold. Apple's lead is structural and durable. Worth bookmarking as a framework piece.

Meta Ray-Ban Display: AR Is About Immersion, Not Occlusion

Stratechery

Ben Thompson finally bought a pair of Meta Ray-Ban Display glasses and it changed how he thinks about the whole AR/VR category. His new framework: the distinction between AR and VR isn't whether your eyes are covered — it's about immersion. Orion and Apple Vision Pro both require you to focus on the device, pushing the real world to your periphery. That's VR, even if you can see the room through it. The Display, with its single-lens HUD and Neural Band wrist controller, does the opposite: you stay anchored in the real world while the interface lives in your peripheral vision. That's true AR.

The AI argument is where this gets interesting. Thompson says the current Display experience is too manual and app-centric — and that's the wrong direction entirely. He wants no app platform: everything should be mediated and ideally anticipated by AI, surfacing the right thing at the right moment. Meta is building toward that; Apple, with its delayed smart glasses and weak AI, isn't. His conclusion: Meta is much better positioned for AR because AR requires AI-first design.

"Everything should be mediated — and ideally anticipated — by AI, showing me what I need when I need it and getting out of the way the rest of the time."

For Hiro: The "ambient AI that surfaces what you need without being asked" vision Thompson describes is exactly the Hiro north star — not a chatbot you summon, but a financial intelligence woven into your day. This is the hardware thesis for that future. Read alongside the Om Malik piece.

  • DeepSeek V4: Late, Behind, and Still China's Best Hope

    ChinaTalk

    DeepSeek V4 finally arrived — and its own authors admit it's "3-6 months behind" state-of-the-art, though ChinaTalk thinks it feels further. A failed training run during migration from Nvidia to Huawei Ascend chips delayed it by months. The hardware architecture story is the real scoop: V4 was still trained on Nvidia but is engineered to run on Huawei Ascend — using a custom DSL called TileLang instead of CUDA. The compute gap is widening: Western labs are training on Blackwells with FP4 support; DeepSeek has been stuck on older Hoppers. Huawei plans to ship 750K Ascend 950 chips this year — that's one week of quality-adjusted American chip production.
