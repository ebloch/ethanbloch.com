Amex Builds the Trust Layer for Agentic Commerce

Fintech Takes

American Express just launched ACE (Agentic Commerce Experiences) — a developer kit plus the industry's first AI agent purchase protection. The framework: register your AI agent with Amex, transmit structured purchase intent, Amex confirms with the cardholder, tokenized payment executes, and if the agent still screws up — Amex absorbs the liability. The closed-loop three-party model (Amex is both issuer and acquirer) is the infrastructure moat that makes this possible.

The deeper play is the liability shift playbook. Just like 3D Secure gave merchants a liability shift in exchange for richer authentication data, ACE will eventually do the same for agent transactions. Right now it's opt-in protection. At some point it becomes 'use ACE or bear the liability yourself' — forcing behavior change across the network.

The open question: will cardholders actually register cards with verified agents and confirm every purchase intent in the Amex app? That friction could kill adoption. But the framework is the right one. Someone had to draw the first line on who's responsible when an AI agent buys the wrong thing.

"The playbook is straightforward: introduce a new capability, attach liability to it, and use that to force a change in behavior among network participants."

For Hiro: This is the core infrastructure problem Hiro will face. When an AI financial advisor takes action on a user's behalf — rebalancing, executing a strategy — who bears responsibility when something goes wrong? Amex is drawing the first lines. The framework (agent registration, structured intent, explicit cardholder confirmation) maps directly to what a trustworthy AI advisor needs to build.

Public Launches AI Portfolio Agents — The Hiro Thesis Goes Live

Fintech Takes

Public just became the first brokerage to offer AI agents that autonomously monitor markets and execute trades based on user-defined strategies. Investors describe what they want, agents set the conditions, and it executes — no manual orders required.

Alex Johnson's take is the sharpest thing I've read about AI in wealth management in months: the best investing strategies optimize for psychology first, intelligence second. DCA works not because it's perfectly optimized but because it removes behavioral mistakes — fear, greed, hesitation. AI agents can preserve DCA's structure while making marginal timing optimizations within a bounded window. "Active without being in the market."

The business model insight is the buried lede: Public monetizes via subscriptions and fees, not payment for order flow. PFOF-based platforms need irrational, emotional humans to keep trading. Robinhood's Vlad Tenev literally said last year he doesn't think AI should do "all your financial planning, all the strategizing for you." Maybe! Or maybe Robinhood just can't afford to let it.

"AI can enable investors to be active without being in the market."

For Hiro: Direct market validation for Hiro's core thesis. Public is the first wealth fintech to ship this — and the subscription model is the reason they could. PFOF misaligns incentives against AI advisors. Worth internalizing as Hiro thinks about its own business model and how to position against incumbents with conflicted revenue models.
