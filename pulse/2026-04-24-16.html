Kalshi & Polymarket Launch Perps — And Blow Up Their Own Origin Story

Fintech Takes

Both Kalshi and Polymarket are launching perpetual futures — leveraged, never-expiring bets on crypto prices that generate continuous funding fees. Alex Johnson at Fintech Takes has the sharpest take on what this actually means: the global perps market did $80 trillion in volume last year, almost all offshore. The CFTC wants to pull that onshore. For Kalshi and Polymarket, it means swapping spikey election-cycle revenue for 24/7 funding-rate fees — a structurally superior business model.

But here's the problem: the public policy argument that got prediction markets their regulatory latitude was that they aggregate real-world information. Perps aggregate nothing — a leveraged bet on Bitcoin's price just shadows the spot market. And the economics of perps actively depend on users losing: platforms earn on liquidations, funding rates, and churn. Kalshi and Polymarket are no longer truth-discovery machines; they're casinos with better PR.

"We price the future. Now you can lever it."

Why it matters: This is a case study in how fintech companies chase better unit economics by abandoning their founding thesis. The civic-goods framing is gone the moment your revenue model depends on users getting liquidated. Worth watching how regulators respond — and whether the CFTC's 'onshoring' ambitions survive this kind of mission drift.

CFPB Kills Disparate Impact Testing — What It Means for AI-Driven Lending

Fintech Takes

The Trump CFPB finalized a rule eliminating disparate impact analysis from fair lending enforcement. Lenders will now only face discrimination claims when there's direct evidence of intentional bias — emails, documents, explicit intent. Statistical analysis of outcomes? No longer sufficient.

The framing from Acting Director Vought was predictably absurd: he called disparate impact testing a "discriminatory race-based policy." But as Alex Johnson explains in his deep dive, disparate impact is precisely what catches unintentional bias in machine-learning models — bias that hides in ZIP code correlations, employment patterns, and hundreds of model features that are individually defensible but collectively harmful. You can build a perfectly well-intentioned model and still produce one that systematically disadvantages protected classes. Outcome testing is the only reliable way to catch it.

The rule also eliminates the "less discriminatory alternative" (LDA) standard — the remediation process that required lenders finding a less biased model achieving the same business objective.

"One hallmark of the second Trump administration has been the eradication of discriminatory race-based policies that have permeated every aspect of government under the banner of 'diversity equity and inclusion'… The administration of fair lending laws is no exception."

For Hiro: Any AI financial advisor that touches credit, product recommendations, or underwriting-adjacent decisions is now operating in a regime with far less regulatory pressure to test for bias. That's a double-edged sword: less compliance burden, but also less accountability if models produce discriminatory outcomes. Worth having a clear internal stance on disparate impact testing — not just for regulatory reasons but for product integrity.

  • Visa + TikTok Launch a Creator Card — and the Payment Stack Behind It Is Wild

    Fintech Takes

    Visa partnered with TikTok on a debit card for UK creators — but the real story is the Rube Goldberg machine underneath: viewers buy Coins ($0.015 each), send virtual gifts to creators during livestreams, which TikTok converts to Diamonds at a ~50% haircut, which creators then cash out. The whole coin-layer exists to avoid money transmitter regulations and the Apple 30% tax. Visa/issuer are likely advancing funds against the settlement lag and absorbing some fraud risk. The product is straightforward; the payment rails behind TikTok's economy are genuinely strange.
