The Anthropic Paradox: Building AI That Might Kill Us (But Won't Build Weapons)

Derek Thompson

Derek Thompson sits down with Anthropic co-founder Jack Clark for a thorny conversation about the company's unusual position: building technology they compare to nuclear weapons while refusing to give the Pentagon unfettered access. The interview gets at the heart of AI's contradictions.

Clark makes a surprisingly nuanced argument: AI isn't like nuclear weapons—it's like a factory that produces everything from microwaves to nukes simultaneously. The company's job is to work with government on the dangerous outputs while letting the productivity tools flow freely. On the employment fears Dario Amodei has stoked? Clark actually pushes back, suggesting job displacement will take longer than his CEO predicts and that we have policy choices to make.

Meanwhile, Anthropic just won a preliminary injunction against the DOD. A judge called the Pentagon's 'supply chain risk' designation of Anthropic 'First Amendment retaliation' for the company's refusal to provide unlimited model access for autonomous weapons.

"AI is fundamentally like everything. It's like a factory that produces cars, micro scooters, animals, and nuclear weapons all at the same time."

For Hiro: This is the existential tension at the heart of Hiro's space. Companies building powerful AI have to navigate between commercial opportunity, national security, and ethical red lines. Anthropic's playbook—cooperate selectively, refuse dangerous applications—is instructive.

Cursor's Secret: Real-Time RL Ships Improvements Every Five Hours

TLDR AI / Cursor

Cursor revealed how they've been iterating so fast on Composer: they're doing 'real-time RL' by serving model checkpoints directly to production, observing user responses as reward signals, and shipping improved versions as often as every five hours. This is not the usual fine-tuning-then-deploy cycle—it's continuous learning in prod.

The implications are significant. If frontier labs and well-resourced startups can close the feedback loop this tightly, the pace of improvement becomes qualitatively different. This may explain why vertical-specific models like Intercom's Fin are beating GPT-5.4 on customer service tasks while costing a fraction.

"The approach allows them to ship an improved version of Composer as often as every five hours."

For Hiro: This is a wake-up call on the infrastructure side. The competitive moat isn't just model capability—it's how fast you can iterate based on real user behavior. Building the feedback loops matters as much as picking the right foundation model.
