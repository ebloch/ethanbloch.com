Elon Musk Found Liable for Securities Fraud Over Twitter Deal

Matt Levine (Money Stuff)

A San Francisco jury found Elon Musk liable for securities fraud for his 2022 tweets about Twitter's bot problem during his acquisition attempt. Musk had signed the $44 billion deal, then tried to back out by publicly claiming Twitter had too many fake accounts—a claim the jury determined was intentionally misleading to shareholders.

The damages are expected to reach $2.6 billion, though that's a rounding error against Musk's $661 billion net worth. The jury rejected two of the four fraud claims but found Musk's 'deal on hold' tweet and bot complaints were designed to manipulate the stock price downward.

"Musk knew all about the bots before signing the deal, Twitter did not lie about them, and the deal was not conditioned on anything at all about bots."

For Hiro: Beyond the obvious market implications, this case tests the boundaries of what executives can say on social media during M&A. For Hiro, it's a reminder that the intersection of social platforms and financial markets creates real legal exposure—something to watch as AI-generated content enters this space.

BlackRock's Larry Fink Warns AI Will Worsen Inequality Unless Everyone Can Invest

Matt Levine (Money Stuff)

In his annual letter, BlackRock CEO Larry Fink argues that AI's economic gains will flow primarily to those who already own financial assets—repeating the pattern of past wealth creation cycles, but at an even larger scale. His solution: make it easier for everyone to invest long-term.

Fink frames this as both a societal challenge and a business opportunity. If AI creates massive economic value (which BlackRock believes it will), the question is who captures it. His implicit answer: expand the investor class through better access to markets.

"The massive wealth created over the past several generations flowed mostly to people who already owned financial assets. Now AI threatens to repeat that pattern at an even larger scale."

For Hiro: This is the macro framing for what Hiro could become: a tool that helps more people participate in AI-driven wealth creation. Fink is essentially making the case for democratized financial advice. My take: the 'universal basic index fund' concept he's hinting at could become a real product category.
