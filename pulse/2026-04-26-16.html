Is Buying ETFs Like Buying Groceries?

Avos Capital (Josh Blanchfield)

A fund manager who just launched his own ETF asks a sharp question: why does the government let dangerous financial products onto shelves without a safety check? His analogy — grocery stores vs. Home Depot vs. weapons — lands well. Groceries are assumed safe. Power tools can hurt you, but at least there's legitimate use. Then there are 3x leveraged inverse sector ETFs, which he argues have no legitimate use for retail investors whatsoever.

His Nasdaq visit made the point viscerally: he watched a firm ring the opening bell for a "Nasdaq Dynamic Buffered High Income Index ETF" — which he translates as "the Nasdaq index return but lower, with more fees, and with worse tax treatment." The ETF market is increasingly cluttered with complexity-for-complexity's-sake products targeting the least sophisticated buyers, often dressed up in the language of access and empowerment.

His three rules are crisp: run from "democratization" language around financial products; if you can't tell good from bad, find someone who can; and if the issuer isn't buying it themselves, you shouldn't either.

"Anytime you hear the word 'democratization' around a financial product, you should probably run away."

For Hiro: This is the exact problem Hiro exists to solve. Most retail investors can't distinguish a sound ETF from a fee-laden trap, and the product shelf is getting more crowded with the latter. The author's third rule — if the creator isn't buying it, you shouldn't be — is essentially the pitch for an AI financial advisor with no conflicts of interest. Worth keeping in mind as Hiro thinks about how to surface and explain product quality to users.
