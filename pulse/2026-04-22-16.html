SpaceX's $60B Cursor Option and The Great AI Coding Land Grab

On my Om

SpaceX has secured an option to acquire Cursor for $60 billion, or pay $10 billion for ongoing collaboration — the latest salvo in the race to own AI coding. Every tier-one lab is now chasing Anthropic's explosive Claude Code success: Sergey Brin has emerged from semi-retirement to lead a DeepMind "strike team" to catch up, and OpenAI just revamped Codex with desktop control and multi-agent workflows.

The numbers tell the story: Claude Code hit a $2.5 billion run rate (doubled since January), dragging Anthropic's total ARR from $14B in February to $19B in March to ~$30B this month. Anthropic's own engineers say Claude Code now writes 70–90% of their code. 4% of all GitHub public commits are now authored by Claude — up from zero eighteen months ago.

Meanwhile, Meta told US employees their computers will record keystrokes, mouse clicks, and screenshots to train "AI agents that can perform work tasks autonomously." On May 20, ~8,000 of those same employees get laid off.

"Marc Andreessen famously said software was eating the world. Software may be eating the world, but looking around the AI landscape the buffet is starting by very much trying to eat its own."

For Hiro: This is the clearest signal yet of where AI value is accruing: coding agents. If you're building an AI company (like Hiro), the playbook is agents + integration + workflow automation. The money follows.

OpenAI and Anthropic May Be Moving Beyond Pure Reasoning

The Information

The Information reports that both OpenAI's upcoming "Spud" model and Anthropic's Mythos suggest the labs are reducing reliance on test-time compute (reasoning chains) while still achieving more intelligent outputs. Early signs point to Spud being a noticeably better pretrained model that answers tough questions without needing reasoning scaffolding.

This matters because reasoning models are expensive — they burn tokens "thinking" before answering. If next-gen models can be smarter out of the gate, it changes the economics of inference significantly.

"OpenAI's upcoming Spud model is noticeably better at answering tough questions without relying on reasoning."

For Hiro: Cheaper, faster inference without sacrificing quality would dramatically change what's possible in real-time financial advice. Watch this space.
