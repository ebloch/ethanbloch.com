Lead Story

Ethan Mollick on Why Interfaces Are the AI Bottleneck

One Useful Thing

Mollick makes a compelling case that the 'AI disappointment' people express isn't about model capability — it's about chatbots being fundamentally the wrong interface for real work. New research shows financial professionals using GPT-4o saw productivity gains offset by cognitive overload: walls of text, sprawling discussions, and AI optimized to be 'helpful' that just mirrors back whatever disorganized structure the user provides.

The piece traces a lineage from Claude Code (built by programmers, for programmers) to OpenClaw (the 'security nightmare' that's now the fastest-growing open source project in history) to Anthropic's new Claude Cowork with Dispatch — which lets you message Claude from your phone while it works on your desktop. Mollick tested it by asking Claude to check if a slide's graph was up-to-date, and it opened PowerPoint, searched his computer, downloaded a PDF with newer data, and updated the slide autonomously.

"People don't want a chatbot. They want an agent that works on their actual files, with their actual tools, accessible the way they talk to people."

For Hiro: This is Hiro's thesis in a nutshell. The financial advisor chatbot is table stakes — the real unlock is an agent that operates on your actual accounts, tools, and documents. Mollick's observation about OpenClaw's success ('talk to AI the way you text people') is exactly the UX pattern Hiro should nail.
