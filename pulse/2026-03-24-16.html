Elon Musk Loses $2.6B Securities Fraud Verdict Over Twitter Deal

Matt Levine (Money Stuff)

A San Francisco jury found Elon Musk liable for securities fraud, concluding he intentionally misled Twitter shareholders with his 2022 tweets about bots and his attempts to back out of the $44 billion acquisition. The damages: $2.6 billion.

Matt Levine's framing is characteristically incisive: Musk's legal arguments were "absolute nonsense" from day one. "Temporarily on hold" isn't a thing for signed public-company mergers. He knew about the bots before signing. The deal wasn't conditioned on bot counts. The jury saw through it.

The award won't dent Musk's $661 billion net worth, but the legal precedent matters. Even the world's richest man can't publicly manipulate stock prices and walk away clean.

"'Temporarily on hold' is not a thing; signed public-company mergers cannot be put 'on hold.' Musk knew all about the bots before signing the deal, Twitter did not lie about them, and the deal was not conditioned on anything at all about bots."

For Hiro: Classic Levine takedown of financial theater. The broader lesson: public markets eventually enforce accountability, even on billionaires. For anyone building in fintech, this reinforces why regulatory compliance and transparent communication matter.

Larry Fink's Warning: AI Will Worsen Inequality Unless We Democratize Investing

Bloomberg / BlackRock

In his annual letter to investors, BlackRock CEO Larry Fink made an unusually pointed argument: AI threatens to make wealthy companies and investors even richer while leaving everyone else behind—unless we find ways to let more people participate in market gains.

He's essentially advocating for broader equity participation—something like universal basic index funds. The challenge is getting individuals to invest for the long-term alongside AI-driven growth.

"The massive wealth created over the past several generations flowed mostly to people who already owned financial assets. Now AI threatens to repeat that pattern at an even larger scale."

For Hiro: This is directly in Hiro's wheelhouse. Fink is articulating the problem Hiro is designed to solve: making sophisticated wealth management accessible to everyone, not just the already-wealthy. When the CEO of the world's largest asset manager is publicly calling for democratized investing, it validates the entire AI financial advisor thesis.
