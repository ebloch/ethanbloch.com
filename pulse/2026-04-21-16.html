Private Markets: Wall Street's Best Trick Is Just Charging More

Bloomberg / Money Stuff

Matt Levine opens with a beautifully simple provocation: the entire job of the financial industry is to funnel money to economic growth — and index funds will do that for you at 0.04% a year. So what are the other guys doing? Charging more. Levine traces the rise of private equity and private credit as a deliberate strategy to shift ownership of the economy into vehicles that can justify higher fees: PE controls 15-20% of the entire US economy at ~2% + 20% of profits, versus 0.04% for a Vanguard index fund. The FT analysis he cites found sixteen private funds alone generated $2B+ in servicing fees for wealth advisers, before upfront commissions.

The structural argument is sharp: financial intermediaries have some ability to decide whether economic growth gets packaged as public or private. That decision may be influenced by fees. It's not a conspiracy — it's incentives. And the retail investor gets steered accordingly.

"So the other main job of the financial industry is to charge more for that."

For Hiro: Direct competitive context. The 1.5% intermediation cost hasn't moved in 125 years despite every technology wave. If AI agents can change that equation, it's a massive opportunity — but understand what you're up against: an industry with deeply embedded incentives to stay expensive.

AI Mania: The Reruns Have Started

WSJ / The Intelligent Investor

Jason Zweig with the market history gut-check: struggling shoe company Allbirds renamed itself NewBird AI and the stock jumped 582% in a single day. Finance professor Michael Cooper has been tracking this playbook — in the dot-com era, companies that added '.com' or 'Internet' to their names got a 53% average short-term boost. When the boom ended, scrubbing those names gave another 64% average boost. The same happened with crypto pivots in 2017. Cooper's new data confirms the pattern is back, this time with AI.

The kicker: the effect is measurable and temporary. These stocks don't stay elevated. The market knows the pivot is cosmetic, but still bids them up. Zweig pairs this with an unexpectedly charming etymology of the word 'hedge' — connecting feudal enclosures to Alfred Winslow Jones coining 'hedged fund' in 1949, via Shakespeare, 17th-century vagrants, and the phrase 'hedge-born swain.' The AI bubble debate has moved from 'is there a bubble?' to 'what specific flavor of bubble is this?'

"Sure enough, it's deja vu all over again!"

For Hiro: The signal in the noise: retail and institutional investors are still price-insensitive to AI hype, which creates both opportunity (real AI products can command premium) and risk (backlash when the names-only pivots collapse). Building something that actually works will matter when the tide goes out.
