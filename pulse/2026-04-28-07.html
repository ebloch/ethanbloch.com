Lead Stories

OpenAI Misses Revenue and User Targets Ahead of IPO

WSJ (via TLDR)

The Wall Street Journal reported that OpenAI missed its own internal targets for both new users and revenue, rattling company leadership about whether it can support its massive spending on data centers. The CFO has reportedly expressed concern that OpenAI may not be able to pay for future computing contracts if growth doesn't accelerate. Board directors have been questioning Sam Altman's push for even more compute capacity in the face of the business slowdown — and executives are now moving to control costs and instill more financial discipline.

The market reaction was immediate: SoftBank fell 9.9% in Tokyo and Oracle dropped ~4% in premarket, as both are key partners in OpenAI's Stargate infrastructure buildout. CoreWeave also fell. The report landed on the same week OpenAI and Microsoft renegotiated their partnership, and just as the Musk trial kicked off — a brutal news cycle for Altman. This is a meaningful signal given the $157B valuation OpenAI raised at last year. The IPO window in Q4 2026 suddenly looks more complicated.

"Board directors have been questioning CEO Sam Altman's efforts to secure even more computing power despite the business slowdown. Company executives are now seeking to control costs and instill more discipline in the business."

Why it matters: This is the internal pressure context for everything happening this week. The missed targets + Musk trial + Microsoft renegotiation all landed simultaneously — painting a picture of a company under real strain as it moves toward a public market debut.

Musk vs. OpenAI: The Trial Begins

Platformer

Jury selection wrapped Monday and opening arguments began Tuesday in Elon Musk's lawsuit against OpenAI and Sam Altman. The fraud claims were dismissed before trial — what remains is Musk's breach of charitable trust and unjust enrichment claims, where he's seeking $150 billion in damages (proceeds going to OpenAI's nonprofit). Several jurors expressed negative feelings about Musk, prompting Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers to note bluntly: "The reality is people don't like him."

Musk has been posting through it all on X, giving everyone nicknames ("Scam Altman and Greg Stockman stole a charity. Full stop"). Meanwhile he boosted the New Yorker's Altman exposé to millions of his followers — effectively doing their PR for them. The stakes beyond the verdict: a $150B adverse judgment — even a partial one — would be catastrophic for OpenAI's path to public markets in Q4 2026.

"We can't wait to make our case in court where both the truth and the law are on our side. This lawsuit has always been a baseless and jealous bid to derail a competitor." — OpenAI

Business & Deals

  • a16z: Workday's Last Workday?

    a16z

    a16z published a detailed investment thesis arguing that HCM software is the last major enterprise category without an AI-native challenger — and that Workday is now structurally vulnerable. The argument: Workday's moat isn't product, it's the 10,500 certified consultants, proprietary Studio tooling, and multi-year contracts. But three things have changed: enterprises are running AI-readiness reviews across legacy systems, coding agents now collapse 12-month implementations into weeks, and Workday literally cannot go AI-native without rebuilding from scratch. The forward-deployed services model owned by the vendor — not Accenture — is what breaks the moat. This thesis maps directly onto the legacy financial software space: "Someone is going to build the next Workday — a system built for agents, not forms and approvals. Once that happens, no one is going back."

Fintech & Policy

  • PACE Act: Congress Moves to Give Fintechs Direct Fed Payment Access

    Fintech Takes

    House Reps Young Kim and Sam Liccardo introduced the PACE Act — creating a new "registered covered providers" designation that would let qualifying nonbank fintechs apply for payments reserve accounts at the Fed, getting direct access to Fedwire, FedNow, and ACH. Requires MTLs in 40+ states and 1:1 reserves. Alex Johnson's read: it's the most state-supervisor-friendly version of fintech payments infrastructure access ever proposed, threading the needle between the OCC's old national fintech charter and the CSBS's opposition.

  • FICO Under Pressure as Fannie/Freddie Move to VantageScore

    Fintech Takes

A 20-year monopoly on mortgage credit scoring is cracking.

Ideas Worth Noting

  • No Jensen, Not All Compute Is Created Equal

    ChinaTalk

    Jensen Huang recently argued that China has enough compute to train frontier AI — "why can't they just put 4x, 10x as many chips together because energy's free?" ChinaTalk's Nick Corvino dismantles this with precision: a Blackwell B200 delivers ~10 petaFLOPs of dense FP8; a typical 28nm automotive microcontroller delivers ~0.12 teraFLOPs. You'd need 2 billion legacy chips to match 100,000 Blackwells. Beyond raw FLOPs, China's chips lack the memory bandwidth (Huawei Ascend 910C uses HBM2E at 3.2TB/s vs Blackwell's 8TB/s) and network bandwidth to train frontier models. The "death by a thousand sub-threshold chips" scenario is largely a myth for AI training specifically.

  • Casey Newton: AI Is Killing the Link Roundup. So Platformer Is Going All-In on Reporting.

    Platformer

    A rare and honest assessment from Casey Newton: he built an automated daily briefing of link summaries using AI, and "it does about as good a job as I do in finding stories of interest, and it does so automatically while I sleep." His conclusion: link aggregation has been commoditized. News analysis is next. Only original reporting — sources, scoops, accountability journalism — survives. So Platformer is dropping its predictable thrice-weekly schedule in favor of publishing when they have something genuinely new to say. A model for any knowledge worker rethinking what AI can and can't replace.
