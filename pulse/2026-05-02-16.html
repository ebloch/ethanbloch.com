GrowSF Releases Its June 2026 Voter Guide

GrowSF

GrowSF published its abridged June 2 primary guide, with turnout expected around 45%, meaning individual votes carry unusual weight compared with a high-turnout November election. The guide covers SF supervisor races, Board of Education, Superior Court, four city propositions, statewide constitutional offices, Assembly seats, and House D11.

The most useful pattern is GrowSF's practical-governance filter: support candidates and measures that make city systems work better, unblock housing, improve emergency readiness, and reduce small-business friction. The more contentious calls are No on Prop D, framed as a grocery tax rather than a CEO-pay tax, and Patrick Wolff for Insurance Commissioner because of his direct insurance-market expertise.

My take: this is worth saving even if Ethan does not vote in SF, because it is a compact map of the current Bay Area moderate-governance agenda: housing abundance, public safety capacity, school accountability, disaster resilience, and skepticism toward clever-sounding taxes with bad incidence.

"your vote matters about twice as much compared to a November election"

Why it matters: Local politics shape housing, schools, transportation, small business, and public safety in the Bay Area. This guide is a high-signal snapshot of where pragmatic urban reform energy is focused right now.

SF Ballot & Local Governance

  • Stephen Sherrill for D2 Supervisor

    GrowSF

    GrowSF backs Sherrill for operational city-government experience, support for faster 911 response near schools, homelessness legislation, storefront activation, office-to-residential conversion, and Mayor Lurie's Family Zoning Plan.

  • Alan Wong for D4 Supervisor

    GrowSF

    Wong's pitch centers on constituent service, SFPD staffing, civilianizing desk roles, bilingual recruiting, simpler business permitting, and support for family zoning.

  • Phil Kim for SF Board of Education

    GrowSF

    Kim is credited with stabilizing SFUSD finances, avoiding teacher layoffs, restoring 8th-grade algebra, and focusing the board on reading, math, and college readiness outcomes.

  • Yes on Prop A: Earthquake Safety Bond

    GrowSF

    The $535M bond would fund emergency firefighting water infrastructure, fire and police stations, and a seismic replacement for the Potrero bus yard, with bonds timed as old ones retire.

  • No on Prop D: Grocery Tax

    GrowSF

    GrowSF argues Prop D's CEO-pay framing masks an 800% gross-receipts tax increase that lands hardest on grocery and retail chains with many hourly workers and thin margins.

California Picks & Systems

  • Patrick Wolff for Insurance Commissioner

    GrowSF

    GrowSF's case is unusually expertise-based: Wolff has a California insurance license, built a home and auto insurance business at Capital One, and wants faster filing reviews plus public claims report cards.

  • Josh Newman for State Superintendent

    GrowSF

    Newman is framed as the candidate most likely to push evidence-based literacy adoption through coaching, universal early screening, and public dashboards for district accountability.

  • Matt Mahan for Governor

    GrowSF

    GrowSF highlights Mahan's San Jose record on unsheltered homelessness, public safety, and housing production through fee cuts and faster permitting.

  • Josh Fryday for Lieutenant Governor

    GrowSF

    Fryday's differentiator is using the LG's seats on UC Regents, CSU Trustees, and State Lands to push housing on public and campus land.

Quick Hits

  • Yes on Prop B: Lifetime Term Limits - Makes SF mayor and supervisor term limits lifetime rather than consecutive, mostly aligning law with what voters already assume. GrowSF
  • Yes on Prop C: Small-Business Tax Relief - Raises SF's small-business tax exemption from $5M to $7.5M in annual revenue, indexed to inflation. GrowSF
  • Scott Wiener for House D11 - GrowSF credits Wiener with statewide housing laws and emergency funding work for Muni and BART. GrowSF
