Stablecoins Are Better Understood as a Settlement Layer, Not a Visa Killer

Fintech Takes

Alex Johnson argues that the common crypto story about stablecoins killing card networks misdiagnoses the payments system. Visa and Mastercard are not just expensive pipes; they coordinate authorization, trust, dispute rules, fraud allocation, and bank participation. The better stablecoin opportunity is where today’s payment system is genuinely weak: cross-border money movement, settlement, and programmability.

The useful frame for Hiro is Chesterton’s Fence. Before declaring a financial rail obsolete, identify which coordination job it performs and which part is merely historical baggage. My take: stablecoins are most interesting when they remove correspondent-bank friction, not when they pretend merchant acquiring and consumer credit rules are free.

"They look at a 2-3% fee, assume it's a pointless fence erected by greedy middlemen."

Why it matters: Directly relevant to fintech product strategy: the winning wedge is not lower fees in the abstract, but replacing the specific coordination costs that incumbents no longer need to own.

The Electricity Market Paid for Savings That May Not Have Been Caused

Bloomberg Money Stuff

Matt Levine unpacks American Efficient, which allegedly sold projected energy-efficiency savings into grid capacity markets by buying sales data and environmental attributes from retailers. FERC says the company collected hundreds of millions for reductions it did not actually cause.

The broader lesson is about markets for counterfactuals. Paying for not using electricity sounds economically clean, but the product depends on attribution, baselines, and measurement. My take: this is the same shape of problem as fintech rewards, carbon credits, and AI evals. If the metric is valuable and hard to verify, someone will optimize the story around it.

"Committing to use one less megawatt at peak times is about as good as committing to supply one more."

Why it matters: A good reminder for any financial or AI product that prices outcomes: the accounting layer is the product, and weak attribution turns incentives into an attack surface.
