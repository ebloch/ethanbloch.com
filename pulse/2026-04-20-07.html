TSMC's CEO Has Seen the Agents Light

Stratechery

TSMC raised its 2026 revenue outlook to above 30% growth and announced it's building entirely new 3nm fabs — one in Taiwan, one in Arizona, one in Japan — a dramatic break from its historical model of never adding fresh capacity to nodes that have already hit their target. The proximate cause: Nvidia's Rubin GPU architecture needs more 3nm than TSMC's existing capacity can supply, and these new fabs are essentially purpose-built Rubin fabs.

But the more telling moment came from CEO C.C. Wei's prepared remarks, where he explicitly named the shift from 'query mode' to 'agentic AI and the command and action mode' as the reason for stepped-up demand. Ben Thompson flags this as extraordinary: TSMC executives almost never discuss end-user applications. Wei doing so suggests Jensen Huang — or someone very persuasive — has convinced him that agents aren't hype, they're a genuine step-change in compute demand.

"The shift from generative AI and the query mode to agentic AI and the command and action mode is leading to another step up in the amount of tokens being consumed. This is driving the need for more and more computation, which supports the robust demand for leading edge silicon."

For Hiro: If TSMC's notoriously conservative CEO is now publicly building the AI agents thesis into his CapEx plan, this is validation at the highest possible level of the supply chain. The infrastructure layer has fully bought in. Agentic AI is not a feature — it's the demand curve TSMC is now building fabs for.

The Private Credit Reflexivity Trap

Verdad

A new HBS paper formalizes something credit veterans have intuited: lending conditions themselves shape default probabilities, creating a self-reinforcing cycle. When defaults are low, lenders get confident, loosen standards, borrowers lever up — which actually keeps defaults low in the near term (easy refinancing), making lenders even more confident. The system looks stable precisely when it's most fragile.

Verdad maps this onto private credit today. Since 2010, private credit has grown from 2% to more than 5% of GDP. More importantly: it now holds roughly 45% of all US high-yield default risk — in a market that's newer and far less tested than bonds or leveraged loans. The historical rhymes are uncomfortable: S&Ls in the 1980s junk bond boom, insurers buying GICs. Today's equivalent: offshore 'Bermuda Triangle' reinsurers and retail-facing BDC funds.

"Lender optimism is self-reinforcing in the short run and self-defeating in the long run. What ultimately matters is whether loans are repaid and losses realized, but that verdict arrives years later."

For Hiro: If private credit is the S&L of this cycle, a repricing event hits wealth management clients hard — exactly the people Hiro is being built for. Worth holding as a mental model alongside any portfolio construction work.
