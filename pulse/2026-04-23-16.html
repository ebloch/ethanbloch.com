Block Is Quietly Building 'Claude Code for Money'

Popular Fintech (via TLDR)

Block is building AI agents — MoneyBot for Cash App, ManagerBot for Square — that don't just answer questions but execute financial workflows: payments, savings, inventory management, operations. The underlying Goose agent framework marks a fundamental shift from reactive chat interfaces to proactive task execution. Block's thesis is that the agent becomes the primary interface, not the app.

The strategic logic is compelling: whoever owns the agent layer owns the customer relationship. Financial data compounds — every transaction, pattern, and preference makes the agent smarter and stickier. Block has proprietary data from Cash App and Square that no newcomer can replicate. That's the moat.

The competitive implication: the race in fintech is no longer about building better apps. It's about who gets there first with an agent that users trust to act on their behalf.

"This strategy positions Block to own the 'agent layer' of financial services, where control over data and workflows compounds into a durable advantage."

For Hiro: This is Hiro's exact competitive space. Block's MoneyBot/ManagerBot framing — proactive agents that own financial task execution — maps directly onto what Hiro is building. The question isn't whether AI financial agents are coming; it's whether Hiro can own a defensible slice of this before the big platforms get there. Block has distribution; Hiro needs a sharper wedge.

Stratechery: Google Cloud CEO Thomas Kurian on the Agentic Moment

Stratechery

Ben Thompson's annual Google Cloud Next interview landed with a pointed question: Gemini was "belle of the ball" four months ago, but lately all the agent momentum has been Anthropic/Claude and Codex. What happened? Kurian's answer was measured but revealing — he let customers speak: Citi is using Gemini agents to run a new wealth advisor product that researches a client's financial priorities and makes portfolio recommendations. Real deployments, at scale, at tier-1 banks.

The structural argument Kurian made: three things have unlocked agents in 2026 — (1) models that reason better, (2) long-running memory, and (3) tool abstractions like MCP that let agents interact cleanly with enterprise systems. Google's integration advantage — Gmail, Drive, Docs, Calendar — is now being weaponized as the agent harness. Gemini Workspace Intelligence stitches all of it together into a "central control layer for business operations."

Kurian also flagged what Google believes is its moat: both Google Cloud and Google the company run on the same infrastructure stack. Every improvement Google makes internally becomes a product feature. That feedback loop is something AWS and Azure can't replicate.

"For Citi, they'll be talking about a new wealth advisor, Investment Management, where they're using our agents to research a person's investment priorities. So a person says, 'Here's my priorities for investment, my kids are going to school, I need this kind of cash flow in order to fund it', and then it researches your financial portfolio and interacts with you to give you recommendations."

For Hiro: Citi is already deploying an AI financial advisor agent through Google Cloud. This is validation that the market Hiro is targeting is real and moving fast — but it also means enterprise cloud incumbents are bringing their full distribution weight to this problem. Hiro's advantage has to be depth of financial intelligence and UX, not infrastructure.

    One Useful Thing (Ethan Mollick)

    Mollick got early access and his verdict is unambiguous: this is a real generational leap. He fed GPT-5.5 a decade of academic research data, four prompts, and zero edits — and got back a paper with a real literature review, real statistics, and sophisticated methodology. The frontier is still jagged on creative coherence, but core reasoning and execution are pulling away fast.
