Nate Silver on Why 'AI Polls' Are Models, Not Data

Silver Bulletin

Eli McKown-Dawson (with commentary from Nate) tackles 'silicon sampling' — startups using LLMs to simulate poll respondents instead of surveying real people. Aaru just hit a $1B valuation doing this, despite their 2024 election predictions showing Harris leading in swing states.

The core argument: polling is fundamentally data collection. Synthetic sampling produces no new data — it's a model predicting what a poll would say. That's a meaningful distinction. The models can replicate topline results but struggle with nuance: they can't get respondents to be 'as racist or sexist or, frankly, as negative as human respondents,' and they miss complex voters like Arizona Trump supporters who also voted to expand abortion.

"If there's a shift in opinion among this subgroup, you're not going to detect it. So if I were running a campaign, I'd invest more in going the extra mile to find a representative sample of those voters. And then I'd hire some smart quants — or Claude? — to figure out the implications."

For Hiro: Direct parallel to Hiro's thesis: AI doesn't replace the need for good data — it makes good data more valuable. The financial advisor space has the same dynamic: AI can model behavior, but the advisors with real client relationships and proprietary insight win. The '$1B valuation for poll predictions' story is a bubble indicator, but the underlying insight about data scarcity is real.
