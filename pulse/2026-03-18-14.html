Yes, AI Is a Bubble. And That Might Be Fine.

Derek Thompson / The Atlantic

Private sector AI spending in 2026 is forecast to exceed $700 billion—as a share of GDP, more than the combined peak spending on the Manhattan Project, Apollo, and Interstate highway construction. Derek Thompson called Paul Kedrosky to push back on his earlier optimism that AI might escape the bubble-then-bust pattern.

Kedrosky's take: 'AI is a bubble. There's no question.' The railroad parallel is instructive—roughly half of track miles built at peak were eventually abandoned, with crashes in 1873, 1878, and 1887. Yet by 1900, railroads were 62% of index market cap. Tech is about 60% today.

The unique danger now: loose credit, real estate, technology, and government policy are all converging. Every actor feels rational—'I don't know what those guys are doing in technology, but we here in real estate, we know what we're doing.' The intersection produces something economically indefensible.

"You can have a hugely valuable buildout that is really consequential for decades in terms of both productivity AND economic and financial carnage."

For Hiro: the question isn't whether AI is transformative but how to build during a bubble without getting wiped out. The 'rational actors creating irrational outcomes' framing applies to any startup competing with hyperscalers. My take: Kedrosky's railroad analogy suggests there's space for focused players who survive the consolidation—you don't need to be Union Pacific to matter.

When Machines Meet Nuclear Command

ChinaTalk

Pranay Vaddi (former NSC arms control director) and Chris McGuire (former State Department nuclear policy lead) discuss the uncomfortable intersection of AI and nuclear weapons. The 2022 Nuclear Posture Review was the first to formally address AI: 'The United States will maintain a human in the loop for all actions critical to informing and executing decisions by the President to initiate and terminate nuclear weapons employment.'

AI is already creeping into NC3 (nuclear command and control)—faster attack detection, targeting recommendations, intelligence fusion. But it introduces new failure modes. Studies show AI is substantially more prone to nuclear escalation than humans in war games.

The most chilling section: McGuire on submarine detection. 'It seems infeasible that we're going to be able to hide a ship that is hundreds of feet long and weighs millions of tons anywhere in the world, given the technical detection capabilities becoming available.' If AI-powered sensors can find our subs, the stable deterrence logic of the Cold War breaks down.

"The whole advantage of AI is parsing signal from noise. Whether it's undersea detection or space-based surveillance, the idea that we can hide these massive things with advanced detection coming online is something we can't bet on."

For Hiro: Not directly Hiro-relevant, but this is the kind of long-term thinking about AI second-order effects that matters. My take: The sub detection piece is genuinely terrifying—it's the kind of capability shift that sounds abstract until suddenly it isn't.
