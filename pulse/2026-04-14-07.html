OpenAI's Internal Memo Reveals Deep Anxiety About Anthropic

Stratechery

Ben Thompson dissects a leaked memo from OpenAI's CRO Denise Dresser that reads like a company trying to convince itself it's winning. The memo leads with "raw capability is no longer enough"—never a promising start when you're supposed to have the best raw capability. Thompson notes OpenAI's compute advantage is real, but Dresser's claim that Anthropic is inflating its revenue by $8B through accounting tricks feels like cope.

The real tell: OpenAI is positioning "Frontier" as its enterprise agent platform, essentially copying Palantir's playbook of deep data integration. They're now on Amazon Bedrock, which Microsoft can't be thrilled about. The memo's pettiest line? "Their story is built on fear, restriction, and the idea that a small group of elites should control AI." That's one way to spin your competitor's safety focus.

My take: When you have to send an internal memo explaining why you're still winning, you're probably not.

"Their story is built on fear, restriction, and the idea that a small group of elites should control AI. Our positive message will win over time."

The Mercor Hack Exposes AI's Most Vulnerable Moat

ChinaTalk

Mercor, a $10B startup that builds specialized human-expert datasets for AI labs, was hacked. 4TB of data allegedly stolen—candidate profiles, video interviews with experts, source code, proprietary information. The alleged price for nonexclusive access? $1 million. For context, Mercor pays contractors over $1.5M per day to build this data.

This matters because expert data is one of the key remaining moats for frontier labs. Chinese fast-followers have already been caught mass-distilling Claude models; now they can potentially access the exact datasets powering the frontier. Meta has already paused Mercor contracts. The piece argues this strengthens the case for stronger export controls and federal security assistance for "national-interest AI startups."

My take: If you can distill models and steal training data, compute becomes the only remaining differentiator. This hack is a bigger deal than the coverage suggests.

"If one can distill models and steal critical expert data, then access to compute becomes an even more important long-term differentiator."
