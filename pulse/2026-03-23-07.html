Notion Abandons Cursor for Claude Code and Codex

The Information

Hundreds of Notion engineers are migrating away from Cursor to Anthropic's Claude Code and OpenAI's Codex. The shift reflects a broader industry move from IDE-integrated tools (autocomplete, inline suggestions) to autonomous coding agents that can complete entire tasks independently.

The division of labor is revealing: junior engineers favor Claude Code for its ability to anticipate intent without precise instructions. Senior engineers prefer Codex for complex, long-running tasks — some run agents overnight for up to eight hours. But Codex requires more careful prompting to avoid going off-track.

The revenue picture tells the story: Claude Code leads at $2.5B annualized, Cursor at $2B, and Codex recently crossed $1B. Cursor's brand is now associated with the old paradigm, and its latest model (Composer 2) got criticized for being a fine-tuned version of Moonshot's Kimi 2.5.

"Many engineers argue that Anthropic and OpenAI are best suited to build the software around their models to get the best coding agents because they know their own models the best."

For Hiro: Direct signal for Hiro: the value is shifting from tools that assist to agents that execute. If financial planning follows the same pattern, the question becomes how to build an agent that can handle complex, long-running financial tasks with minimal hand-holding.

a16z: There Are Only Two Paths Left for Software Companies

a16z

In a blunt memo to software CEOs and boards, a16z declares the comfortable middle is over. Public markets have repriced software because terminal value isn't what it used to be. The firm sees only two credible paths to durable equity value:

Path One: Accelerate revenue growth by 10+ points through genuinely new AI-native products in the next 12-18 months. Not chatbot bolt-ons — real products that move the needle. Put 50% of R&D on net-new AI products, use four-person pods, collapse design/product/engineering, cap headcount not compute.

Path Two: Rebuild for 40%+ true operating margins (including stock comp) within 12-24 months. This means flattening management, standardizing implementation, killing committees, raising prices where you own the workflow, and treating every share issued as a real expense.

"Somewhere in your org, there are ~five people who are going to deliver you 100x the amount of value you ever thought possible. Your first job is to figure out who these five people are (no matter how junior they are on paper!), explain the urgency of the situation with them, and give them the career opportunity of a lifetime to rebuild the company with you."

For Hiro: This is the strategic lens for evaluating any software company — including Hiro. The 'four-person pod' model for AI product development is worth studying. My take: this memo is really about the coming agent disruption; if agents can do the work of 10 engineers, you need fewer engineers and more people who can direct agents effectively.
