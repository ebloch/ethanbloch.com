The Maduro Trade: How One Soldier's Polymarket Bet Broke Prediction Market Regulation Wide Open

Bloomberg / Money Stuff

Army special forces soldier Gannon Van Dyke was charged with making $400K+ on Polymarket by betting on Nicolás Maduro's removal — using classified intel he had as part of Operation Absolute Resolve, the actual military mission to capture Maduro. He VPN'd into Polymarket (technically off-limits to US traders), placed 13 bets on "Maduro Out" contracts, and watched them pay off.

The insider trading charge is almost the boring part. The wilder story is the legal theory the CFTC is using: Polymarket isn't registered with the CFTC, but it doesn't matter — because "event contracts" are "swaps" under the Commodity Exchange Act, and swaps are within CFTC jurisdiction. Full stop. That means the CFTC arguably has regulatory reach over all prediction markets, registered or not. Matt Levine's reading goes further: if event contracts are swaps, then technically any bet — on sports, elections, anything — might be a commodity subject to insider trading law.

For fintech, this is a sleeper regulatory bombshell. The prediction market space just got dramatically more complicated, and any product that looks like "betting on outcomes" — including some fintech ideas around financial hedging or insurance — is now clearly in CFTC crosshairs.

"If you want to do some insider trading on top-secret military information, you might prefer Polymarket to Kalshi, as Van Dyke allegedly did."

Why it matters: Prediction markets touching fintech/financial products are squarely in Ethan's world. This case just established that the CFTC has jurisdiction over unregistered event contracts — a precedent that ripples into any fintech product that involves outcome-based payouts.

Abundance: Instacart Founder's AI Hedge Fund Actually Tries to Replace Portfolio Managers

Bloomberg

Apoorva Mehta — Instacart co-founder — launched Abundance, an AI-first hedge fund in Palo Alto. Thousands of AI bots scour the internet for trade ideas, conduct research, pick stocks, size positions, and execute trades. The goal is to eventually have AI run the entire fund; some strategies are already fully AI-run today.

Matt Levine's dry skepticism cuts to the heart: "I will be impressed when a hedge fund uses AI agents to raise money from investors without human involvement. Trading the stocks is the easy part!" The harder observation is the architectural question — Abundance's bots read the internet (natural language, news, forums) rather than being trained on financial time-series data. That's a fundamentally different bet: that general language intelligence applied to stocks beats purpose-built financial models. Jury's very much still out.

This matters because it's the clearest current data point on "what does an AI-native financial firm actually look like in practice?" Abundance is building the hedge fund equivalent of what Hiro is building in personal finance.

"Thousands of bots scour the internet for trade ideas, conduct in-depth research, pick stocks to wager on and against, size bets and even execute trades."

Why it matters: Direct comp to Hiro's thesis — what does AI-native financial decision-making look like? Abundance is the agent-led hedge fund; Hiro is the agent-led personal advisor. The architectural choices here (internet-trained vs. financial-data-trained agents) are the same design questions you're wrestling with.

  • Jain Global Collapses Into Millennium — A Cautionary Tale on Pod Shop Economics

    Bloomberg

    Bobby Jain's much-hyped hedge fund launch is over: Jain Global is returning all external client capital and will manage money exclusively for Izzy Englander's Millennium. The culprit is textbook pod shop math — in 2025, the fund made $750M in trading profits but investors saw only ~25% of it after fees and expenses, netting a 3.7% return while peers posted double digits. This is the structural trap of starting a multistrategy pod shop: employees want huge cash pay on day one, investors want steady returns, and the runway is brutally short.

  • Commerce.com's Board Rejected a 47% Discount Takeover — and Rezolve Thinks Its Stock 'Should' Be Worth 4x More

    Bloomberg

    Rezolve AI ($1B market cap) made a hostile all-stock offer for Commerce.com ($250M market cap) at a 47% discount to market price — arguing that analyst price targets of $11 (vs. its actual $2.88 trading price) make its shares worth far more, so shareholders should be happy to swap at a loss. Commerce.com's board said no. Levine's verdict: "I have never previously seen a public company argue that, because its $2.88 stock should be worth $11, it can be used to pay for $11 worth of acquisitions." A genuinely new flavor of financial absurdity.
