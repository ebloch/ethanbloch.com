Lead Stories

Oil Prices Have a Ceiling — And Governments Are the Counterparty

Doomberg

Despite everything that happened in the Iran war — the Strait of Hormuz closure, attacks on Qatar's LNG facilities, actual missiles flying — WTI crude never breached the highs from Ukraine, let alone the 2008 peak. On an inflation-adjusted basis, the current peak was half of 2008's print.

Doomberg's takeaway is stark: if you're betting on energy price spikes, the government is effectively your counterparty. Reserves were released, significant jawboning occurred, and mountains were moved to cap prices. This will happen every time.

Meanwhile, Australia had just 30 days of oil reserves when the conflict started (IEA policy requires 90). The disconnect between energy security rhetoric and actual preparedness was laid bare.

"In times of energy shortages, if you are betting on increasing prices, you should know that the government is effectively your counterparty to the trade. The authorities don't want energy prices to skyrocket and will do whatever is required."

Why it matters: Important mental model for macro thinking: energy price spikes are political emergencies, and governments will intervene. This limits the upside of any energy-crisis trade. The shale co-production dynamic (oil drilling → gas oversupply → negative Permian gas prices) also has implications for anyone thinking about natural gas exposure.
